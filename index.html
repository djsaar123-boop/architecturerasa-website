A Case for Verandahs

Ar. Amit Rasa · 2025 · Vernacular · Social Living

Ar. Amit grew up between villages and cities. Having seen the breadth of rural homes alongside dense urban neighbourhoods, he studied what traditional houses actually do — how they hold light, mediate between family and stranger, and support different modes of living through the day.

"The verandah is not wasted space. It is where life actually happens."

In the growing conversation around Vernacular Architecture, the studio is interested in one question: can the verandah and courtyard — long associated with bungalows and havelis — be adapted for apartment buildings with constrained floor plates?

Residential Farmhouse, Rajasthan — open-plan interior with garden court

Traditional Indian homes operated as nested social ecosystems: an outermost verandah, a semi-private courtyard, and inner rooms. This gradient shaped how people moved, conversed, and rested through the day. The studio is working on a modular typology that brings some of that structure to multi-storey housing. The work is ongoing.

Stone, Shadow & Breeze

Ar. Amit Rasa · 2024 · Heritage + Microclimate · Jaipur

Jaipur summers are harsh. In peak heat, surface temperatures breach 47°C. And yet inside an older haveli, the air can drop by 6–8 degrees without mechanical cooling. The answer is stone, geometry, and the Jali.

"The Hawa Mahal was a calibrated climate machine — 953 windows, each placed with care."

This project applies that principle to a contemporary plot: south-facing, covered on three sides. Rather than fight the orientation, the studio introduced a stone Jali screen as a secondary skin, separated from the wall by an air cavity. Hot air rises through the gap, drawing cooler air inward at ground level.

Facade study — perforated screen and air cavity

Thin slits in the roof deck allow morning light to travel from roof to ground floor — tracking across the floor through the day. The work is half-implemented. Residents already report a difference. A thermal study is planned on completion.

Cave Café: Designing for Privacy

Studio Notes · 2024 · Bandra West, Mumbai

The brief was to make a café feel like your own corner — not a space designed for visibility. Mumbai cafés are usually optimised for throughput. This client wanted the opposite: the sense of being sheltered.

"The cave — not as darkness, but as shelter. The feeling of being held."
Cave Café, Bandra West — arched alcoves and oval skylights

Individual alcoves along the perimeter, each with its own light pocket and a slight level change, create micro-territories within the larger volume. The oval oculi bring in diffused light. The green checkered marble floor, warm teak, and dusty pink upholstery build a sense of depth unusual for a mid-size hospitality space.

Evening — bar counter and arched windows to the Bandra skyline

The arched windows along the western wall open to the city at dusk, backlighting tables without direct glare. You can see the street. The street cannot quite see you.

The Studio

Ar. Amit Rasa

Founder · Architecture Rasa · Jaipur

Ar. Amit Rasa grew up between a village and a city. That experience gave him an early interest in how architecture changes depending on who is using it and what they need from it. The practice is named for the Sanskrit concept of essence — the feeling a space leaves behind.

The studio works across residential, hospitality, and institutional projects, alongside ongoing research into vernacular building traditions and their relevance to contemporary climate challenges.

Current Research

Based in Jaipur with active projects in Mumbai, Nashik, and Rajasthan. We take a limited number of projects each year.
