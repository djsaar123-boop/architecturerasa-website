A Case for Verandahs

Ar. Amit Rasa  ·  2025

Ar. Amit grew up between villages and cities. Having lived with the breadth of rural homes and the density of urban neighbourhoods, he became interested in what traditional houses actually do — how they hold light, mediate between family and stranger, and support different rhythms through the day.

"The verandah is not wasted space. It is where life actually happens."

In the growing conversation around vernacular architecture, the studio is working on one specific question: can the verandah and courtyard — long associated with bungalows and havelis — be adapted for apartment buildings with constrained floor plates?

Residential Farmhouse, Rajasthan — open-plan interior with garden court

Traditional homes operated as nested social ecosystems: a semi-public verandah, a semi-private courtyard, and inner rooms. This gradient shaped how people moved, conversed, and rested. The studio is exploring modular setbacks that restore some of that social structure to multi-storey housing. The work is ongoing.

Stone, Shadow & Breeze

Ar. Amit Rasa  ·  2024 · Jaipur

Jaipur summers are harsh. Surface temperatures breach 47°C. And yet inside a pre-colonial haveli, the air drops by 6–8 degrees without any mechanical cooling. The answer is stone, geometry, and the Jali.

"The Hawa Mahal was a calibrated climate machine — 953 windows, each placed with care."

This project applies that principle to a contemporary urban plot: south-facing, covered on three sides — ordinarily a difficult orientation by both solar logic and vastu. Rather than fight it, a stone Jali screen was introduced as a second skin, separated from the primary wall by an air cavity. Hot air rises through the gap, drawing cooler air inward at ground level — a Venturi effect born from geometry alone.

Facade study — perforated Jali screen and air cavity, Jaipur

Thin horizontal slits in the roof allow morning light to travel from the roof slab to the ground floor — even in a plot that would otherwise be in permanent shadow. The work is half-implemented. Residents already report a difference. A full thermal study follows on completion.

Cave Café: Designing for Privacy

Studio Notes  ·  2024 · Bandra West, Mumbai

The brief was simple: make a café feel like your own corner. Mumbai cafés are usually optimised for throughput and visibility. This client wanted the opposite — the sense of being sheltered within the city rather than exposed to it.

"Not darkness — shelter. The particular feeling of being held."

Cave Café, Bandra West — the main hall, oval skylights, and arched alcoves

Individual alcoves along the perimeter, each with its own light pocket and a slight level change, create micro-territories within the larger volume. Oval oculi in the ceiling bring in diffused natural light — like forest canopy, like spotted monsoon cloud. The deep green checkered marble floor, warm teak, and dusty pink upholstery build a sense of geological depth unusual for a city café.

Evening — bar counter and arched glass to the Bandra skyline

The arched windows along the western wall open to the city at dusk, backlighting every table in warm hour without glare. You can see the street. The street cannot quite see you.

The Studio

Ar. Amit Rasa

Founder & Principal · Architecture Rasa · Jaipur, Rajasthan

Ar. Amit Rasa grew up between a village and a city. That experience gave him an early interest in how architecture changes depending on who uses it and what they need from it. The practice is named for the Sanskrit concept of essence — the feeling a space leaves behind.

The studio works across residential, hospitality, and institutional projects, with ongoing research into vernacular building traditions and their relevance to contemporary climate in India.
